# Research Sources Guide

**Purpose**: Comprehensive guide to finding reliable sources for Tech Teardown research.

---

## Primary Sources

### Company Documentation
- **Company Archives**: Official company archives (if accessible)
- **Product Documentation**: Original manuals, spec sheets, press kits
- **Press Releases**: Official announcements and product launches
- **Annual Reports**: Company financial reports from the era
- **Internal Publications**: Company newsletters, internal memos (if leaked/published)

**Where to Find**:
- Company websites (check archive.org for historical versions)
- SEC filings (for public companies)
- Library special collections
- Museum archives

### Patent Filings
- **USPTO**: United States Patent and Trademark Office
- **European Patent Office**: For European patents
- **Google Patents**: Easy search interface
- **Patent Applications**: Often reveal early design thinking

**Why Useful**: Shows original design intent, technical details, competitive landscape

### Trade Show Materials
- **CES Archives**: Consumer Electronics Show materials
- **Comdex**: Computer trade show (historical)
- **Photokina**: Photography trade show
- **NAMM**: Music/audio trade show

**Where to Find**:
- Archive.org
- Museum collections
- Trade show websites (historical)

### Original Advertisements
- **Print Ads**: Magazines, newspapers
- **TV Commercials**: YouTube, archive.org
- **Trade Publications**: Industry magazines

**Why Useful**: Shows marketing positioning, target audience, era context

## Source Quality Checklist

### Reliable Sources
- ✅ Primary sources (original documents)
- ✅ Peer-reviewed academic papers
- ✅ Established publications
- ✅ Museum collections
- ✅ Official company documentation

### Use with Caution
- ⚠️ Wikipedia (check citations)
- ⚠️ Forums (verify claims)
- ⚠️ Personal blogs (verify facts)
- ⚠️ YouTube videos (check sources)

### Always Verify
- ❌ Uncited claims
- ❌ Single-source information
- ❌ Anecdotal evidence (unless clearly labeled)
- ❌ Speculation presented as fact

## Citation Best Practices

### What to Cite
- Direct quotes
- Specific facts and figures
- Historical claims
- Technical specifications
- Dates and timelines

### How to Cite
- Include source name
- Include publication date
- Include URL (if online)
- Include access date (for web sources)

### Example Format
```
According to Byte Magazine (March 1984), the device featured...
As documented in the original press release (Company Name, 1984)...
As noted in the Computer History Museum collection...
```

## Tips for Effective Research

1. **Start Early**: Begin research before acquiring device
2. **Take Notes**: Document sources as you find them
3. **Verify Facts**: Cross-reference important claims
4. **Follow Citations**: Wikipedia citations lead to sources
5. **Use Archive.org**: Historical website versions
6. **Check Multiple Sources**: Don't rely on single source
7. **Document Everything**: Save URLs, dates, quotes
8. **Ask Communities**: Forums have knowledgeable members
9. **Be Skeptical**: Verify claims, especially dramatic ones
10. **Enjoy the Process**: Research can be fascinating!

## Common Research Challenges

### Challenge: Limited Information
**Solution**: 
- Check archive.org for historical websites
- Look for contemporary reviews/articles
- Check forums for user experiences
- Contact museums or collectors

### Challenge: Conflicting Information
**Solution**: 
- Prioritize primary sources
- Check publication dates (earlier often more accurate)
- Look for consensus across multiple sources
- Note discrepancies in research

### Challenge: Finding Primary Sources
**Solution**: 
- Use archive.org for historical websites
- Check patent databases
- Look for scanned magazines on archive.org
- Contact museums or archives

### Challenge: Verifying Anecdotes
**Solution**: 
- Look for multiple sources
- Check if story appears in multiple places
- Verify key facts independently
- Present as "reportedly" if unverified
